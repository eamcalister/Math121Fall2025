    2.

    Similarly, we can take limits as \(x \) approaches a finite value, \(a.\) In this case, instead of taking larger values of \(x \) to get close to \(L ,\) we take values closer to \(a .\) Specifically,
    ðŸ”—
    Definition: If we are given a function \(f\) and a number \(a \) (which could be \(\pm \infty \)), we say that \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \) if we can make \(f(x) \) as close to \(L\) as we would like by taking values of \(x\) sufficiently close to, but not equal to, \(a \) (Note that \(L\) can also be \(\pm \infty .\)) In other words, \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \) means that as \(L \) is the \(y-\)value \(f \) approaches as \(x \) approaches \(a, \) irrespective of the value of \(f \) at \(a.\)
